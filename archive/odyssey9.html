# Odyssey 9 (2024/10/07)

In Book 9 of Homer's poem The Odyssey, Odysseus has received food and drink from the Phaeacians, and after stating his name, he begins telling them a story as a gift--and as a way to manipulate them into giving him many gifts and a safe passage home. His description of his encounter with a cyclops provides multiple stories in one: on the surface, he uses rhetoric to make himself a hero, and beyond that, a story of theft and greed.

I am Odysseus, son of Laertes, known to the world for every kind of craft--my fame has reached the skies. (21-22)

Having lost everything in a rough sea journey, Odysseus's goal is to build himself up in the eyes of the Phaeacians, gain many gifts from them, and return home as a successful hero. To do so, he relies on their assumptions, or "warrants", as Professor Alexander describes in the Humanities Core handbook, of how civilized people should act. By portraying the cyclops as brutal and uncivilized, Odysseus provides an opposite example and strengthens the bonds between him and the Phaeacians, while simultaneously turning himself into a hero.

Left: a depiction of Odysseus recounting his story to the Phaeacians, by August Malmström in 1853

A grim loner, dead set in his own lawless ways. Here was a piece of work, by god, a monster. (210-211)

Through his carefully-chosen descriptions of the cyclops Polyphemus, Odysseus justifies his actions, which include stealing the cyclops' cheese and sheep, deceiving him, and blinding him.

Right: ancient pottery depicting Odysseus' crew blinding Polyphemus, from the Archaeological Museum of Argos

# Host and Guest

Xenia is the Greek ritual of hospitality, whose stages are meant to be followed in a specific order.

Odysseus uses this to his advantage through the story, hoping to both illustrate the cyclops' uncivlized nature and inspire the Phaeacians to be courteous to him.

However, analyzing his own actions reveals that he, too, disrespected Xenia multiple times.
